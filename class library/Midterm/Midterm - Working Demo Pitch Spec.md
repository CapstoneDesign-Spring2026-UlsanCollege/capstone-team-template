# MIDTERM - Working Demo Pitch Spec

## Purpose
The Midterm is an **investor-style pitch** supported by a **working demo**, a **brochure**, and **repo evidence**.

Your job is to convince the audience that:
- the problem is real
- the users matter
- your solution makes sense
- your team has made real progress
- your team has a believable path to a finished product by the Final

This is **not** a generic class presentation.

---

## Format
Each team gets:
- **8 minutes** for the pitch
- **2 minutes** for Q&A

If your live demo fails or stalls, you must switch to your backup within **15 seconds**.

---

## Audience
Present as if you are speaking to potential investors or supporters.

That means your presentation should focus on:
- the problem
- the users
- the product
- what works now
- why it is worth supporting
- what comes next

Do **not** assume the audience knows your code, stack, or repo structure.

---

## Required Components
Your Midterm presentation must include all of the following:

### 1. Problem
Explain the problem clearly and specifically.

### 2. Target Users
Explain who the product is for.

### 3. Solution
Explain what the product does and why this solution makes sense.

### 4. Core Features
Show the most important features only. Prioritize clarity over quantity.

### 5. Current Progress
Show what is real right now.

### 6. Demo
Show one main user flow or one tightly related set of features.

### 7. Timeline / Roadmap
Show:
- what will be completed by the Final
- what will likely happen after the Final

### 8. Team Roles
Show who is on the team and what each person is responsible for.

### 9. Ask
End with a simple, believable ask.

Examples:
- pilot users
- funding for next-stage development
- mentorship
- technical support
- promotion / partnerships

The ask should match your project and current stage.

---

## Demo Requirement
You must show:
- a **working live demo**, or
- a **backup demo video / narrated screenshot walkthrough**

### Demo rule
Your demo should focus on **one main core flow**.

Do **not** try to show every feature.

### Good demo examples
- one user can sign up, log in, and complete the main action
- one donor can post an item and another user can request it
- one user can search, view, and complete the main task

### Bad demo examples
- too many disconnected features
- random clicking
- explaining future features instead of showing current ones
- “please imagine this part works”

---

## Brochure Requirement
Each team must prepare a **brochure-style project handout**.

It can be:
- printed,
- digital with a QR code shown at the beginning of the presentation,
- or both.

This is **not** just your slide deck in another format.

The brochure must be its own communication piece.

---

## Team Participation Requirement
All team members must:
- be present
- participate visibly
- have a defined presentation role

Speaking is encouraged for most members, but not every member must speak if they are clearly responsible for another visible presentation role.

### Acceptable participation examples
- presenting one section
- running the demo
- introducing the brochure
- handling the QR code handoff
- explaining the roadmap
- answering Q&A
- switching to the backup demo if needed

### Not acceptable
- standing silently with no visible role
- appearing on the team slide only
- one student doing everything

---

## Q&A Requirement
Be ready for investor-style questions after the pitch.

The instructor may continue asking questions until time is up.

You should be ready to explain:
- the problem
- the users
- the product
- what is actually built
- what still needs work
- why your roadmap is realistic
- what support you would want next

---

## Recommended Pitch Structure
A simple structure that works:

1. Hook / project intro
2. Problem
3. Users
4. Solution
5. Core features
6. Current progress
7. Demo
8. Timeline / roadmap
9. Team roles
10. Ask

---

## Common Mistakes
Avoid these:
- talking too much about plans and not enough about real progress
- trying to show too many features
- giving a vague problem statement
- reading the slides word-for-word
- showing a broken demo with no backup
- forgetting to explain what each team member does
- making an unrealistic timeline
- ending without an ask

---

## What Good Looks Like
A strong Midterm pitch is:
- clear
- focused
- believable
- supported by proof
- easy to follow
- professionally delivered
- realistic about what is done now and what comes next

The goal is not perfection.

The goal is to show a project that feels **real, progressing, and worth supporting**.
